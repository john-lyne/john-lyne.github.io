## Old Testament

### Genesis, First book of Moses

#### Creation

- Earth begins without form and void, water deep and dark with Spirit flowing over the water’s face
- Then God says ‘Let there be light’. Notice the language ‘let there’: he _allows_ light to be. Then he _separates_ light and darkness, and the light was good. Unity to multiplicity.

- To allow multiplicity the delight in gathering back into unity? In becoming like God?

- Further separation of waters with the sky (2nd day), then with land and plants (third day), then celestial realm: sun, moon, stars (fourth day), water animals (fifth day), land animals according to their kinds and humans, made in the image of God (6th)
- “Be fruitful and multiply, and fill the earth and subdue it…” and have dominion of all moving creatures, plants.

- Subdue it…apprehend it? Use it? Exploit it? But also fill the earth… so not just exploitation, and not just apprehension. Fill and subdue. Create and exploit. Generate and moderate(?). Implicate and explicate.

#### Creation and The Fall

- Man formed out of dust, and the breath of life (spirit) was breathed into his nostrils and a soul was born.
- Placed in the garden of Eden, where the tree of life and the tree of knowledge of good and evil lay.
- “Now the serpent was more subtle than any other wild creature that the Lord God has made…”
- Once the forbidden fruit was eaten, their “eyes were opened, and they know that they were naked.” Self-consciousness unlocked. The focus on vision seems important…especially referential, also perhaps the most obvious, and thus potentially misleading sense (i.e., prone to false confidence)?
- God says that, as punishment, Eve will: bear pain in child birth, desire her husband, and he will rule over her (for she misbehaved without said rule). She will also battle against the snake (evil) for time to come.
- Man is punished by being doomed to “in toil…eat of [the tree of knowledge of good and evil] all the days of your life; thorns and thistles it shall bring forth to you; and you shall eat plants of the field.”

- Plants of the field: earthly, material knowledge.

- The woman is given a name: Eve, meaning something like “living”. Adam and Eve are both then clothed by God.
- God said “Behold, the man has become like one of us, knowing good and evil”. Man is made in likeness to God, although this time by defying God? Pre-fall, was man more like God?
- Man drove out of the garden. In the east of the garden, a cherubim and flaming sword turning every which way guard the way to the tree of life.

- East: sunrise—flaming sword is like the sun. The tree of life is guarded by youth (cherubim) and, according to some, reason (flaming swords). The flaming swords slice yet illuminate, divide yet unite, like the mind. Revolve in every which way: open awareness, yet centered on whomever wields the swords (God?). To approach the tree of life, we must become like those who guard it: we must become like children, and sharpen and brighten our minds (not in a ‘knowledge’ way (that would be just a sword), but more in an ‘understanding’ way.)

#### Cain and Abel

- Born of Eve, the older brother Cain (a tiller of ground) offers fruit to god, the younger Abel (a keeper of sheep) offers the firstlings of his flock. The Lord regard Abel’s offering but not Cain’s. “If you do well, will you not be accepted? And if you do not do well, sin is lurking at the door; its desire is for you, but you must master it.”
- Cain kills his brother in spite. God punishes him: the earth will no longer yield to him its strength and he shall be a fugitive and wanderer of the earth. Materially bound, wandering aimlessly.
- Cain is marked so that nobody kills him. Branded as a miscreant, but also branded as someone not allowed to be killed…why? We must leave that to god?

#### Adam’s descendants to Noah

- Adam has a son Seth, to replace Abel, when he is 130 years old. He has more children and lives 930 years. Seth has children, and lives 912 years.
- Bunch more generations, then Noah is born, who will “bring us relief from our work and from the toil of our hands.”

#### The wickedness of mankind, Noah’s ark

- Men took to wives such as they chose (alluding to irresponsible choice of mates) and mankind multiplied. Men’s lifespan reduced to 120 years. Wickedness greatened, and evil was flowing within the thoughts of man’s heart. The lord was sorry he made man, and said he would blot man out, and all creatures.
- But Noah was righteous, blameless, and walked with God.
- God floods the earth for forty days and nights. Noah gathers his immediate family and two of each kind of animal on the ark. It has a roof, therefore it floats as the waters rise.
- Eventually the water stops, having risen higher than mountains. Upon the highest mountain, the ark settles first as the water drains (provoked by God sending wind across the deep (alluding to first creation story?)).
- Noah sends out a raven to see whether the land dried. It goes to and fro but doesn’t return. He sends out a dove: it first returns for there was still water; after seven days he tried again and the dove returns with an olive leaf in its beak; and after seven more she does not return.

- Interesting. The dove is a she. Also wonder if Jesus’ first miracle is related to the flood: loads of water this time being a cause of elation rather than destruction.

- “Whoever sheds the blood of man, by man shall have his blood shed.”
- “Noah was the first tiller of soil. He planted a vineyard; and he drank of the wine, and became drunk, and lay uncovered in his tent.”

- Water to wine…

- One of his three son’s snitches on him when he’s naked and drunk, but the two other sons cover him. Canaan is doomed to slavehood under the other two brothers.

- Wonder if Canaan people betrayed another nation when that nation was vulnerable.

#### The Tower of Babel

- Babel = Babylon. They were a greatly wealthy empire at the time.
- Nimrod, a might hunter before the lord, establishes Babel. Its people build a tower that extends to the heavens to avoid being scattered upon the earth and to make a name for themselves (pride).
- The god acknowledges their oneness, their one language, that this is just the beginning of what they’ll do and nothing will be impossible for them (hmmm…). Then he makes it so they cannot understand one another and scatters them about the earth.
- Babel is close to the Hebrew balal, which means confused.

#### The call of Abram

- Abram is called to leave his country, his kindred, his father’s house, and in return he will bless him and those who bless Abram.
- Abram goes off, gets told Canaan will at some point become his so he builds an altar there and goes on, until he hits Egypt. His wife is beautiful so he tells her to call him his brother so he will not be killed. She marries the Pharaoh, who gets cursed, and returns her to Abram who has accumulated some wealth. Off they go.
- Abram settles in Canaan. He wins some battle and is honoured by the king of Salem and priest of the most high, Melchizedek, who brings bread and wine for him, blessing and praising him.
- Abram gives Melchizedek a tenth of everything. The king of Sodom, after the war, asks for the conquered people but offers all the goods to Abram. Abram declines, and suggests his family take their share.
- Abraham’s wife cannot conceive, therefore his wife encourages him to marry his maid. He has a son with her.
- Abram = exalted one. Abraham = father of a multitude.
- God is about to lay waste to a city, and Abraham asks him to spare it if such and such a number of righteous people lie in it. He works down to 10 people. Somewhat reminiscent of the 10 percent of his wealth he gave to Melchizedek.

#### The immorality of Sodom

- Lot lives in Sodom, where he lets two angels stay with him for the night. They warn they may bring trouble, but Lot insists. The sodomites arrive and demand he show them who he’s housing, but he refuses and attempts to protect the angels. The sodomites burst past him and into his home against his will.
- Lot later escapes and Sodom is rained in fire and brimstone in response to the disrespect. (Perhaps a volcano eruption occurred around this time)

#### God tests Abraham

- God tells Abraham to sacrifice Isaac (“he who laughs”), his only begotten son (minus the one with the slave). Isaac carries the sacrifice wood, akin to Jesus carrying the cross. Isaac asks where is the sacrificial lamb, Abraham says “God will provide himself the lamb for a burnt offering, my son.”
- Abraham is about to kill his son when an angel stops him, and recognizes him as truly god fearing. A ram appears behind him with its horns caught in a thicket, which he offers instead.
- A second angel appears, sent by the Lord, and says Abraham will be blessed with descendants as numerous as sand on earth and stars in the sky.

#### The birth of Esau and Jacob

- Rebekah marries Isaac and has twins. Two nations divided are born in her womb, one is stronger than the other, the elder serves the younger. Esau is the elder, red and hairy, and Jacob comes out holding Esau’s heel (Jacob = he who supplants, he who takes by the heel)
- Esau is favored by his father Isaac for he hunts good game, Jacob is favored by his mother Rebekah for he is quiet and smooth.
- One day Esau is famished and asks Jacob for food. Jacob offers it in exchange for Esau’s birthright. Starving, Esau accepts.
- Another, Rebekah overhears Isaac asking Esau to bring him game, as he is blind and nearing death. She tells Jacob, who at first is reluctant, to pretend to be Esau and bring him game instead. He does and is blessed. Esau comes later with game, but his father had already gave away his blessing. He gets told he will dwell away from the fatness of earth and heaven on high, will live by his sword and serve his brother, but will at some point break loose by breaking the yoke at his neck
- Esau plots the murder of Jacob, so Rebekah tells Jacob to flee. He does. Esau marries a Canaanite out of spite, despite his father telling Jacob not to do so.
- Jacob has a dream about a ladder ascending to heaven, strewn with angels of God upon its steps and God himself sitting above/beside it.

#### Jacob wrestles at Peniel

- When Jacob is heading back home after taking to wives, worried about encountering Esau, he encounters a man at a stream. They wrestle, and when Jacob doesn’t let up, the man touches Jacob’s thigh and puts it out of joint. The man asks Jacob to let him go, but Jacob refuses unless he blesses him. Jacob is blessed and renamed Israel (He who strives with God).
- Jacob asks the man his name, but the man responds “Why is it that you ask my name?”, blesses him, and off he goes.

- The namelessness of God.

#### Joseph dreams of greatness

- Joseph (He who adds), Rachel’s first son, is most loved by his father Jacob. Joseph has a dream that he was a sheave upright and all his brothers were sheaves that bowed to him. He told his brothers and they were very jealous. He told his father a similar dream and his father rebuked him, but kept the saying in mind.
- Joseph is a dreamer, like his father. His brothers refer to him as the dreamer.
- Joseph is skilled at interpreting dreams. He successfully predicts the dreams of the pharaoh’s butler and baker when they are thrown in prison with him. When they complain of having nobody to interpret their dreams, Joseph says: “Do not interpretations belong to God? Tell them to me, I beg you.”

- Interpreting dreams as godly. Metaphor. The bridge.

- Genesis ends with Israel’s (Jacob’s) death, and Joseph forgiving his brothers for betraying him, stating that “you meant evil against me: but God meant it for good, to bring about that many people should be kept alive, as they are today. So do not fear; I will provide for you and your little ones.” Joseph eventually dies in the final paragraph.

### Exodus, second book of Moses

#### Birth and youth of Moses

- Named Moses for he was “drawn out of water”

#### Moses and the burning bush

- “And the angel of the Lord appeared to him in a flame of fire out of the midst of a bush; and he looked, and behold, the bush was burning, yet it was not consumed.”
- When God saw that Moses turned aside to see why the bush wasn’t burnt, God recognized him and called out to him.

- Recognizing wonder and majesty is rewarded? To recognize that spirit that flows through all things, burns in all things, everlastingly?

- “Do not come near; put off your shoes from your feet, for the place in which you are standing is holy ground.”

- Shed thy pride.

- “And Moses hid his face, for he was afraid to look at God.”

- God-fearing. Reminds of Arjuna with Vishnu in his cosmic form

- “When you have brought forth the people out of Egypt, you shall serve God upon this mountain.”

- Eternal service?

#### God reveals his name

- God says to Moses his name: “I Am Who I Am”. Say this to the songs of Israel, ‘I Am has sent me to you.’

- I am… being…

- God instructs Moses to say to the Pharaoh: “The Lord, the God of the Hebrews, has met with us; and now, we beg you, let us go a three days’ journey into the wilderness, that we may sacrifice to the Lord our God.”
- Moses receives three gifts from God. 1) His staff, his rod, that becomes a serpent unless he takes it by the tail (the rod of God); 2) when he puts his hand in his chest, it comes out white as snow, and when he puts it back and pulls it out, his flesh is returned; 3) if he takes water out of the Nile and pours it upon dry soil, it becomes blood.

- 1) taming of evil through uprightness, order, a trusting grasp, subduing cunning; 2) hmm… something to do with heart giving/taking; 3) water into blood… inanimate into animate… material into vital

- God then grants Moses eloquence of speech to help convince his people and the Pharaoh of God’s demand.
- When Moses and Aaron face the Pharaoh and he asks of them a miracle, Aaron casts down the rod of God and it becomes a snake. The Pharaoh’s wise men and sorcerers then did the same by their secret arts, however Aaron’s rod swallowed up their rods.

- Those who imitate are bound to be devoured by that which they imitate.

- The Pharaoh’s heart was still hardened, so Moses struck the Nile with his rod and turned it into blood, foul and undrinkable, killing the fish in it. “There shall be blood through all the land of Egypt, both in vessels of wood and in vessels of stone.”

- Hmmm, similar flavour as Christ’s first miracle, but life-taking rather than life-giving

- The magicians reproduced this trick as well by their secret arts, so the Pharaoh’s heart remained hard.
- The first plague was the water-blood, the second frogs, the third gnats. The magicians couldn’t reproduce this one… hmm… why? Too fine?
- Fourth was flies, fifth death of livestock, sixth boils. In this one it says “But the Lord hardened the heart of Pharaoh, and he did not listen to them; as the Lord had spoken to Moses.”

- The Lord hardened the Pharaoh’s heart? Huh? Wouldn’t he be working against himself here? If it’s not the Pharaoh hardening his own heart, what agency has he?

- Seventh is thunder and hail. The hail destroys the earth of Egypt, and the Pharaoh once more asks Moses to stop the onslaught, that he will let the Israelites go. Moses says he will stop it, and so he does. But the Pharaoh once again lies. Moses knows this: “But as for you and your servants, I know that you do not yet fear the Lord God.”
- The eight: locusts. “Go in to Pharaoh; for I have hardened his heart and the heart of his servants.” So the Lord is hardening their hearts, so that he may unveil more of his potential?
- Ninth is darkness, tenth is Passover. Lambs are sacrificed by Israelites, their blood is painted on the doorposts. Only unleavened bread shall be eaten for the next 7 days. God passes over the blood painted houses and the firstborn in all of Egypt are killed.
- The Israelites are allowed to leave. They first venture through the wilderness, towards the Red Sea. The pharaoh has his heart hardened and chases after the Israelites. As the Egyptian army is closing on the Israelites, they begin to doubt. “For it would have been better to serve the Egyptians than to do in the wilderness.” Moses replied “The Lord will fight for you, and you have only to be still.”

- Stillness. Faith.

- Moses strikes his staff and God parts the Red Sea with an east wind. The Egyptians follow, the Lord clogs their chariot wheels, and they are enveloped by the sea after Moses is instructed to wave his hand over it. All are killed.
- Amid the deep, fluid unknown, the Israelites found solid ground by grace of their Lord and their belief in Him. The Egyptians still doubted, were still questioning the Lord’s profundity and oceanic potential, and so they were forced to face it. Testament to faith.
- The Israelites continue to move through the wilderness, and went three days without water. There was only bitter water nearby, and after crying to the Lord Moses is instructed to throw a tree into the water, and the bitter water is made sweet.

- Hmmm… mysterious.

- After delivery of the Ten Commandments upon mount Sinai, the people tremble. But Moses says to them, “Do not fear; for God has come to test you, and that the fear of him may be before your eyes, that you may not sin.”

- Interesting… do not fear, but in any case your fear of god is to stop you from sinning.

- Moses goes up to Mount Sinai for forty days/nights, and God gives him a h bunch of “shalls”: how to build the ark of the covenant, all the accessories, priestly garments, ordination ceremonies, stuff like that. While he’s away the Israelites become restless. To appease them, Aaron tells them to offer him their gold, and moulds it into a golden calf. They worship it. God becomes furious, but Moses asks him to repent of his evil and remember his covenants with Abraham, Isaac, and Israel. God repents.
- Moses admonishes the Israelites for the false worship. He tells the sons of Levi that they must kill their son and brother to atone for their false worship (explaining a civil war?). Then God hits them with a plague.
- The Lord says to Moses: “you cannot see my face; for man shall not see me and live." And the Lord said, "Behold, there is a place by me where you shall stand upon the rock; and while my glory passes by I will put you in a cleft of the rock, and I will cover you with my hand until I have passed by; then I will take away my hand, and you shall see my back; but my face shall not be seen."

- Mysterious. We may only see the rear of God. He takes away His hand… revelation. _Allowing_ us to see but a part of Him.

- The Lord proclaims to Moses: “the Lord, a God merciful and gracious, slow to anger, and abounding in mercy and faithfulness, keeping merciful love for thousands, forgiving iniquity and transgressions and sin, but who will by no means clear the guilty, visiting the iniquity of the fathers upon the children and the children’s children…”

- Inheritance of iniquity. Similar to karma.

- “…for you shall worship no other god, for the Lord, whose name is Jealous, is a jealous God…”

- Hmmm… seems insecure.

- God renews the covenant with Moses, another forty days, and Moses takes the stone tablets (again, he’d broken them before in frustration of the golden calf mishap) down to the people. This time his face is glowing after having spoken with God. This freaks the people out, so he veils his face when speaking with them, and unveils it when he goes to speak with God.
- Moses finishes his work on establishing the tabernacle, ark, all that stuff. Then the cloud hung over and the glory of God filled the tabernacle. In day the cloud was upon it, at night fire. The cloud and fire, the glory, represent the presence and transcendence of God. (?)

### Leviticus, third book of Moses

- "When you reap the harvest of your land, you shall not reap your field to its very border, neither shall you gather the gleanings after your harvest. And you shall not strip your vineyard bare, neither shall you gather the fallen grapes of your vineyard; you shall leave them for the poor and for the sojourner: I am the Lord your God.”
- “When a stranger sojourns with you in your land, you shall not do him wrong. The stranger who sojourns with you shall be to you as the native among you, and you shall love him as your-self; for you were strangers in the land of Egypt: I am the Lord your God.”

#### Punishment for disobedience

- If we break from God, He “will appoint over you sudden terror, consumption and fever that waste the eyes and cause ife to pine away. And you shall sow your seed in vain, for your enemies shall eat it; I will set my face against you, and you shall be struck down before your enemies; those who hate you shall rule over you, and you shall flee when none pursues you. And if in spite of this you will not listen to me, then I will chastise you again sevenfold for your sins…and your strength shall be spent in vain, for your land shall not yield its increase, and the trees of the land shall not yield their fruit.”

- The terror, depression rings a modern bell. “You shall flee when none pursues you.”

- “…if you walk contrary to me…I will let loose the wild beasts among you, which shall rob you of your children…and make you few in number, so that your ways shall become desolate.”

- Reminds of the suite of bizarre ideologies contributing to childlessness.

### Numbers, fourth book of Moses

- Two men in the village are graced with the Lord’s spirit and begin prophesying. “My lord Moses, forbid them,” says his minister. But Moses replies “Are you jealous for my sake? Would that all the Lord’s people were prophets, that the Lord would put his spirit into them!”
- Aaron and Miriam speak against Moses. God replies “Hear my words. If there is a prophet among you, I the Lord make myself known to him in a vision, I speak with him in a dream. Not so with my servant Moses, he is entrusted with all my house. With him I speak mouth to mouth, clearly, and not in dark speech: **and he beholds the form of the Lord**. …”
- The Israelites are on the move in the wilderness, and once more complain to the Moses for bringing them out there. They are parched for water. Upon being lambasted with these complaints, Moses and Aaron fall on their faces at the door of meeting. The Lord instructs Moses to take the rod and tell the rock to yield its water. Moses and Aaron then gather the people to the rock and say “Hear now, you rebels, shall we bring forth water from this rock?” And Moses lifted his hand and struck the rock twice, and water poured forth abundantly.
- The Lord then said to Moses and Aaron “Because you did not believe in me, to sanctify me in the eyes of the sons of Israel, therefore you shall not bring this assemble to the land which I have given them.”
- Interesting. This is the second ordeal at the rock of meribah. The first time around the Lord instructs Moses to strike the rock and water pours out (Exodus 17). This time around, at the same rock, He tells Moses to *tell* the rock to yield its water, but Moses instead strikes it *twice* and asks the assembly “shall *we* bring forth water from this rock?” Something defiant and prideful about Moses and Aaron’s composure this time around. This may be why God admonishes them after for not believing in Him, otherwise they would have followed his direction. Aaron dies soon after as punishment on Mount Hor, no longer graced with reaching the promise land.

#### Balaam, his donkey, and the angel

- Story of a foreign prince of Moab who is asked to investigate the Israelites who are still in the wilderness making their way to the promise land.
- God is upset with Balaam intruding upon the Israelites, so He manifests in angelic form holding a sword. Balaam cannot see the angel, but his donkey can. First encounter she avoids the armed angel standing on the road, and Balaam strikes her. Second, she sees the angel in a narrow vineyard path and rubs against the wall. Again, Balaam strikes her. Finally, the angel of the Lord stands ahead in a narrow place where there is nowhere to turn. The donkey lay down (submitted) under Balaam, and with anger kindled he strikes her.

- Donkey as a symbol of conscience, of that humble part of us that can feel (but not see?) God. The rider a symbol of arrogance. The gradual narrowing of the path and encounter: our pride eventually forces us into confrontations with the divine.

- The Lord speaks through the donkey “What have I done to you, that you have struck me these three times?” Balaam replies “Because you have made sport of me. I wish I had a sword in my hand, for then I would kill you.” The donkey: “Am I not your donkey, upon which you have ridden all your life long to this day? Was I ever accustomed to do so to you?”

- God as the donkey that supports us. Or as the mediator to God (God speaks through her).
- Also the irony of Balaam wishing he had a sword to slay his donkey (something like God), while the angel holds one.

- The Lord then opens his eyes to his angelic form, holding the sword. Balaam falls on his face and asks for forgiveness. God: “Why have you struck your donkey three times? Behold, I have come forth to withstand you, because your way is perverse before me; and the donkey saw me, and turned aside before me these three times. If she had not turned aside from me, surely now I would have slain you and let her live.” Balaam replies “I have sinned, for I did not know that you stood in the road against me…”

- Acknowledgement of sin, of missing the mark post revelation.
- We always ride upon the donkey (must be some significance in her being feminine). Something we always ride upon, that turns us away from wrongful paths, that sees god, that submits to god, even as we remain prideful.

- Balaam is then ordered only to speak as God wills. He later continues to prophesy four oracles related to the establishment of the promise land.

- Interesting how the man who couldn’t see god, post-revelation, becomes an oracle, a seer.

- “He’s an uncommon love for the common man and godly wisdom resides in the least of things so that it may well be that the voice of the Almighty speaks most profoundly in such beings as lives in silence themselves.”

### Deuteronomy, fifth book of Moses

- “…your little ones, who you said would become a prey, and your children, who this day have no knowledge of good or evil, shall go in there, and to them I will give it, and they shall possess it.”

- It being the promiseland. The innocence of the child, the return to Eden via lack of knowledge of good and evil, and a trust in God.

- “And you came near and stood at the foot of the mountain, while the mountain burned with fire to the heart of heaven, wrapped in darkness, cloud, and gloom. Then the Lord spoke to you out of the midst of the fire; you heard the sound of words, but saw no form; there was only a voice.”

- Wow, the symbolism. The mountain (inspiration), the fire to the heart of heaven (soul, spirit, heart), wrapped in clouds (ignorance, uncertainty), God speaks to us through this burning we have, but through no form, only voice (conscience)

- The formlessness of God is then used to remind Israelites not to worship Gods with form, be it creatures, people, or planets.
- Otherwise, God will become jealous and scatter them. And in their scattering, they will find themselves upon gods of wood and stone, and eventually long for the true, formless god. And God will be merciful in remembrance of His covenant with Noah, Abraham, et al.
- “Out of Heaven he let you hear his voice, that he might discipline you; and on earth he let you see his great fire, and you heard his words out of the midst of the fire.”

- Despite the formlessness, He still revealed Himself to them. Although this fire is a form in a way, they do not worship the fire itself, but the source of the fire.

- The Great Commandment: “The Lord our God is one Lord; and you shall love the Lord your God with all your heart, and with all your soul, and with all your might.”
- “And you shall destroy all the peoples that the Lord your God will give over to you, your eye shall not pity them…”

- Not good.

- God warns Israelites not to claim that it is because of their righteousness that God is granting them victories, but because of their enemies’ wickedness.

- Goes on to remind them all the times they rebelled against god. “You have been rebellious against the Lord from the day that I knew you.” Haha

- “You shall not boil a kid in its mother’s milk.”

- Single line out of nowhere. Out of pocket fr
- Could be referencing a baby goat. Could be a deep metaphor for not using something life giving contrary to its purpose (to take away life). Can be applied to many things: e.g., using religion (that which binds) to stoke hatred and division
- Devouring mother also comes to mind

- “…you shall lend to many nations, but you shall not borrow; and you shall rule over many nations, but they shall not rule over you.”

- Bit of a double standard there on the lending/borrowing. Though maybe it’s not lending with interest, being dependable rather than dependant.
- Ah, explicitly stated in (23:19) that lending to fellow Israelites must be interest free, but to foreigners you may charge interest.

- “For the poor will never cease out of the land; therefore I command you, You shall open wide your hand to your brother, to the needy and to the poor, in the land.” (14:11)
- If a king is ordained by God, “he shall not multiply wives for himself, lest his heart turn away; nor shall he greatly multiply for himself silver or gold.”
- “A woman shall not wear anything that pertains to a man, nor shall a man put in a woman’s garment, for whoever does these things is an abomination to the Lord your God.”

- Oof

- “The secret things belong to the Lord our God; but the things that are revealed belong to us and to our children for ever, that we may do all the words of this law.” (29:29)
- The word is not in heaven, nor beyond the sea, “but the word is very near you; it is in your mouth and in your heart, so that you can do it.” (30:14)
- “For when I have brought them into the land flowing with milk and honey, which I swore to their fathers, and they have eaten and are full and grown fat, they will turn to other gods and serve them, and despise me and break my covenant.” (31:20)
- Moses about to die, will die on Mount Nebo, again “because you broke faith with me in the midst of the sins of Israel at the waters of Meribath…because you did not revere me as holy in the midst of the sons of Israel.” (32:51)

### Book of Joshua

- Before the war in Jericho, Joshua (who takes Moses spot after his death) has a vision of a sworded spirit descending to him and claiming he will command the army in the name of the lord. He instructs Joshua to “put off your shoes from your feet for the place you stand is holy.” (5:15) Callback to Moses and the burning bush.
- A harlot (Rahab) saves some Israelite spies by hiding them. She and her family are spared when Jericho is decimated. The rest of the town and occupants are utterly destroyed.
- The Gibeonites clue in that Israel is wreaking havoc and will inherit the earth, and therefore submit to them proactively. They end up working as slaves, hewing wood and drawing water for the congregation.

- Similar flavour of moral justification as the intouchables, lower castes in Hinduism. Eternally bound to slavehood.

- “I gave you a land on which you had not labored, and cities which you had not built, and you dwell therein; you eat the fruit of vineyards and oliveyards which you did not plant.” (24:13)
- Joshua unites all the hebrews (i.e., those who remained in Canaan(?), such as Esau’s tribe, while Jacob’s boys went to Egypt. He commands them to ditch any other gods they may have come to worship in their time away from one another, and worship Yahweh, the true Israeli god.

### The book of judges

- The Israelites conquer the canaanites, almost. But not quite. So they still mingle with them, and end up worshipping their gods. The Lord sends judges to remind them of the covenant, and the lord is with the judges, but once the judges die the people regress harder. The Lord deserts them in their blasphemy, and instead of wreaking havoc he lets them either undue themselves, or find their way back. This seems like the first relaxing of control—letting them find their way. The training wheels are off.
- Samson’s story is interesting. Born of a woman who is urged by a spirit of god repeatedly to not drink wine or unclean food, for she shall bear a special child.
- Samson, a Nazarite, picks a non Jew as a wife. His parents are hesitant, but apparently god wanted a reason to lay waste to these outsiders. So Samson marries her.
- There is a mystical encounter with a lion, and Samson kills it with his bare hands. When he returns to where he killed the lion, its torn-open body is full of honey and a swarm of bees.

- Symbolic of pride/anger and indulgence?

- Wife ends up betraying him, loses a bet cause she snitched, and she is given to a man who had been his best man.
- Samson burns a bunch of crops by lighting three hundred fox tails on fire and then running off, kills a bunch of people, and slays a thousand more men with a donkey’s jawbone
- Then he marries a Gazite, Delilah, who keeps trying to betray him to give him to the Gazites. He eventually tells her how to make him weak after lying the first few times: to cut his hair. She cuts it all off and he loses his power (Nazirite).
- He is blinded, and he finally dies in a sort of coliseum, not before taking down some columns on his way out, killing three thousand more.
- On Micah, who makes some personal shrines of worship: “In those days there was no king in Israel; every man did what was right in his own eyes.” (17:5)
- Story of a Levite who has a concubine from Bethlehem, Judah. She runs off in anger back home, he goes to get her, his father in law is very hospitable (almost too hospitable). Eventually, off the Levite goes, passing through a Benjaminite city. They do not acknowledge the sojourner, until another who has a house there passes by.
- The Benjaminites are suspicious of the Levite sojourner, and end up raping his concubine overnight. The Levite ends up cutting his defiled concubine into 12 pieces and spreading a peice to the 12 tribes. They end up turning against the Benjaminites, destroying them in response to their blasphemy, commandment-breaking. In pity, and so that the original 12 tribes are all still alive, the 12 tribes give some ladies (though not theirs) to a few (400) Benjaminite survivors, who complain but eventually comply.
- Again, final lines: “In those days there was no king in Israel; every man did what was right in his own eyes.” (21:25)

### The book of Ruth

- Short one. A Bethlehem couple and their two sons sojourn to Moab. The sons marry, but eventually all the men die. The widow is left with her Moabite daughters in law. In misery she returns to Bethlehem, and urges the daughters to go back to their homes in Moab.
- One daughter in law, Ruth, refuses, standing by Naomi (the widow) “for where you go I will go, and where you lodge I will lodge; your people shall be my people and your God my God: where you die i wil de and there will I be buried. May the Lord do so to me and more also if even death parts me from you." (1:16-17)
- Ruth exemplifies the faith of a gentile, a non-Jew, and is eventually rewarded by marrying Boaz after she works diligently in his fields, and he recognizes her devotion. Boaz is of close kin to the widow.

### The first book of Samuel

- Samuel enters priesthood quite young, because his mom prays to the lord. He works under Eli, whose sons blaspheme and are eventually killed. The ark gets stolen by the philistines and the blind Eli falls over in shock and breaks his neck.
- The ark eventually finds its way back after causing some pestilence to those who carried it.
- The people demand Samuel, the latest judge, for a king. He advises against it at first: they’d be subjecting themselves to tax and slavery. But they persist. It is a humble yet attractive benjaminite, Saul, who is eventually chosen by God to rule over the people. Saul becomes prince.
- Saul becomes king. Really, this is to appease the masses. The Lord did not want monarchy—He constantly helps the Jews escape monarchies (e.g., Egypt). Reluctantly, he gives the a king, but reproaches them for wanting one and not trusting the Lord. Says that so long as love for the Lord is number one for the people and king, things will be ok.
- While warring with the philistines, Saul makes a rash oath to his people to not eat until evening. They toil all day, and happen upon a forest with honey on its ground. They resist, but his son Jonathan didn’t hear the oath, dips his staff in the honey, and licks. His eyes light up. The people sack a town and in their hunger eat the meat with blood (big no-no). Jonathan also eventually falls into misfortune.
- Samuel instructs Saul to attack some nation, and to make sure they kill absolutely everyone and every being. Saul does so, but spares the ox and sheep for sacrifice, and spares the foreign king. This belligerence causes him to lose his kingship. Obeisance is valued by God more than sacrifices; Saul listened to his people (who didn’t want to waste resources) rather than the Lord (who deemed those resources impure)
- “Man looks on the outward appearance; but the Lord looks on the heart.” (16:7)
- David and Goliath takes place here. Saul falls out of favour with the Lord, but continues to rule. David, a young shepherd boy, the youngest of eight sons, is chosen as next heir, unbeknownst to Saul. David defeats Goliath with his sling; Saul at first gives him armour before battle, but David takes it off. He’s used to being free, and has sling lions and bears while protecting his flock. The flock he protects are now the Israelites; the lions and bears their enemies.
- Saul becomes jealous of David, for the Lord was with David and not him, and plots to kill the shepherd boy who has now become a commander of good esteem among Israelites.
- “David rose from beside the stone heap and fell on his face to the ground, and bowed three times; and they kissed one another, and wept with one another, until David recovered himself.”

- David upon hearing the king wants him dead, from the king’s son Jonathan. The two are bros, soul-bound. This crying and kissing gives Homeric energy

- Saul slaughters the priest who helped David, and many more.
- David has a few opportunities to kill Saul, but spares his life both times. In the moments, Saul recognizes David’s virtue, but eventually Saul returns to jealousy.
- Saul sees a medium in disguise (who he’d banished out of the land, along with priests). Samuel’s spirit speaks to him, foretelling his doom. Saul collapses, terrified.

### Book of Ezra

- Book of Kings ended with an exile of the Jews from Jerusalem, by the Babylonians, due to a series of degenerate kings. Book of Ezra opens with their return to Jerusalem thanks to the generosity of some Persian kings. They are allowed to rebuild their temple there, although the locals who occupied the land after they’d left got jealous and hampered the build for a few decades. The locals asked to participate in the build and join the religion, but the Jews declined.
- Eventually, king Darius finds in his royal archives the original decree to allow the Israelites to build their temple, and issues an order for them to recommence the build.

- An example of a religiously tolerant culture (Persia) encouraging the continuation of a more reserved, closed-off religious group (monotheistic Judaism). Admirable expression of charity. Would the Jews have done the same for others? I guess they had many times, but it was abominable and they were consequently punished for false worship. Those who built the altars of Baal, for instance, on Israel’s land, who did not crush their altars, were disgusting and brought misfortune on their people. Something doesn’t seem quite right here… a double standard.

- Intermarriage forbidden. Bunch of doxxing of Israelites who had foreign wives.

### The Book of Tobit

- Tobit’s a nice guy, helps out the poor and deceased Jews, and is mocked for it. He falls asleep outside and bird droppings fall in his eyes and he goes blind. Desolate, he prays for death at the same time as another, Sarah, since a demon keeps killing her prospective husbands.
- An angel Raphael is sent disguised as a human, and ends up escorting Tobit’s son Tobias to get some money left with a relative. Tobias ends up running into Sarah, they get married, they pray before sleeping together, and he burns a magic fish heart to exorcise the demon.
- They return, and Raphael uses the magic fish gall to cure Tobit’s blindness.
- Charity, faithfulness, and suffering core themes of this story. Faithfulness doesn’t necessarily reduce suffering, but makes it fertile. Faith fertilizes suffering.

### Book of Judith

She makes herself pretty and walks out to the army, they take her in, she demonstrates her wisdom and charm, the general takes a liking to her, gets drunk one night, she cuts off his head, the army flees, Judith is celebrated. ### The Gospel According to John

**
#### Chapter 1

**

**1** In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God. **2** He was in the beginning with God; **3** all things were made through him, and **without him was not anything made that was made**. **4** In him was life, and the life was the light of men. **5** The light shines in the darkness, and the darkness has not overcome it.

- Without + not anything made, double negative. The dual apophatic reveals something more than the mere assertion.
- To be without him, to be without the Unity, is to be without creation, to not participate in the making of things.

- As Augustine was without God in his money-and-status-chasing days. He didn’t really create anything, just moved around what already was.

**10** He was in the world, and the world was made through him, yet the world knew him not.

- The whole affording part, but the part not affording the whole. The material built of the spiritual, but the spiritual built of something beyond the material. Gestalt. Asymmetry.

  

**18** No one has ever seen God; the only Son,who is in the bosom of the Father, he has made him known.

- Through the Son, the foreground of the divine background, we come to know the ineffable, we are led to the ‘God no one has even seen.’

  

#### Chapter 3

**3** Jesus answered him, “Truly, truly, I say to you, unless one is born anew [or from above], he cannot see the kingdom of God.”

- Metanoia as well. Same idea as repentance, turning one’s mind to the One Truth, akin to a rebirth in consciousness.

  

**8** The wind blows where it wills, and you hear the sound of it, but you do not know whence it comes or whither it goes; so it is with every one who is born of the Spirit. … **12** If I have told you earthly things and you do not believe, how can you believe if I tell you heavenly things?

- Christ to Nicomedus, the ruler of the Jews. Again, the spiritual cohering with the material, but the material not necessarily cohering with the spiritual.

  

**31** He who comes from above is above all; he who is of the earth belongs to the earth, and of the earth he speaks; he who comes from heaven is above all.

- Descent from heaven, manifested in body, return to heaven. Spirit to material to spirit. Implicit explicit implicit. Enfolded to unfolded to enfolded. Through this is further created, further given.

  

**34** For he whom God has sent utters the words of God, **for it is not by measure that he gives the Spirit**; **35** the Father loves the Son, and has given all things into his hand. **36 He who believes in the Son has eternal life**; he who does not obey the Son shall not see life, but the wrath of God rests upon him.

- Not by measures is the Spirit offered. Pure selfless offering, unconditional.
- To believe in the One is to be at-one with eternity.

  

#### Chapter 4

  

**13** Jesus said to her, “Every one who drinks of this water will thirst again, **14** but whoever drinks of the water that I shall give him will never thirst; the water that I shall give him will become in him a spring of water welling up to eternal life.”

- Again, the material and unalive vs. the spiritually alive. From the finite we thirst again, but the unmeasurable fount is ceaseless, One, throbbing with continuous, eternal life.

  

**24** God is spirit, and those who worship him must worship in spirit and truth.

- To worship in spirit is to worship in God, for God is spirit. Again, pure ceaseless surrender.

  

#### Chapter 5

**16** And this was why the Jews persecuted Jesus, because he did this on the sabbath. **17** But Jesus answered them, “My Father is working still, and I am working.”

- God never ceases his Work. Endlessly and effortlessly he stirs the universe. Although this may be contested by Genesis, God had rested before, and rest, negation, withdrawal can be as generative as affirmative work.
- This claim that God ceased working is still…conceptual, an abstraction. But the purity of experience suggests a ceaselessness.

  

**19** Jesus said to them, “Truly, truly, I say to you, the Son can do nothing of his own accord, but only what he sees the Father doing; for whatever he does, that the Son does likewise.

- Complete surrender to the will of the Father.

  

**22 The Father judges no one,** but has given all judgment to the Son, **23** that all may honor the Son, even as they honor the Father. He who does not honor the Son does not honor the Father who sent him. **24** Truly, truly, I say to you, he who hears my word and believes him who sent me, has eternal life; he does not come into judgment, but has passed from death to life.

- The implicit judges no one, but the explicit does. The explicit is afforded by the implicit (the Father sent the Son). To believe in the Father, interestingly, is to also believe in the Son and avoid the Son’s judgement, and this belief in the implicit Father is akin to belief in the eternal, that which affords and is beyond time. This is life, freedom from the discrete (through the discrete Son to the Father, bewilderingly).

  

**29** and come forth, those who have done good, to the resurrection of life, and those who have done evil, to the resurrection of judgment.

- The resurrection of self giving love (life itself) and divisive judgement (death itself)… resurrection of death. Not even what seems like death can be destroyed!
- Resurrection, to rise again! Resurrection of judgement is like a rising again of what falls. A sort of earthly cycle of rebirth.

  

**39** You search the scriptures, because you think that in them you have eternal life; and it is they that bear witness to me; **40** yet you refuse to come to me that you may have life.

- Scriptures are insufficient to atone with God, it is through a life with Him, the only God, that we find life.

  

**44** How can you believe, who receive glory from **one another** and do not seek the glory that comes from the **only** God?

- To receive glory from one another is to worship the finite and divided and perishable. This is idolatry. Rather seek the glory that affords the ‘one anothers’, that being the Unity within which the multiplicity whirls.

  

**47** But if you do not believe his writings, how will you believe my words?

- His being Moses’. Scripture still matters.
- Again the idea of having a good understanding of the immanent and material as a prerequisite (?) of getting in touch with the spiritual, but not getting caught in the material.

  

#### Chapter 6

**26** Jesus answered them, “Truly, truly, I say to you, you seek me, not because you saw signs, but because you ate your fill of the loaves. **27** Do not labor for the food which perishes, but for the food which endures to eternal life, which the Son of man will give to you; for on him has God the Father set his seal.”

- An appeal to surrender from material rewards. Seek not a reward for oneself, seek only to be united with Him, to believe in Him.

**63** It is the spirit that gives life, the flesh is of no avail; the words that I have spoken to you are spirit and life.

  

#### Chapter 7

**16** So Jesus answered them, “My teaching is not mine, but his who sent me; **17** if any man’s will is to do his will, he shall know whether the teaching is from God or whether I am speaking on my own authority. **18** He who speaks on his own authority seeks his own glory; but he who seeks the glory of him who sent him is true, and in him there is no falsehood.

- It is not I who teaches myself, but others, and fundamentally He by whom all others are taught.
- Seek not personal glory, but the glory of that which gave rise to your personhood.

  

**38** He who believes in me, as the scripture has said, ‘Out of his heart shall flow rivers of living water.’”

  

#### Chapter 8

**4** they said to him, “Teacher, this woman has been caught in the act of adultery. **5** Now in the law Moses commanded us to stone such. What do you say about her?” **6** This they said to test him, that they might have some charge to bring against him. Jesus bent down and wrote with his finger on the ground. **7** And as they continued to ask him, he stood up and said to them, “Let him who is without sin among you be the first to throw a stone at her.”

- The writing with his finger in the ground (a sort of living law) against the ossified law of Moses. Nobody threw the stone, for the law in their hearts, their conscious, that living law within, told them not to harm her.

  

**15** You judge according to the flesh, I judge no one.

- Contradiction with earlier claims of the Son judging, the Father not judging.

  

**26** I have much to say about you and much to judge; but he who sent me is true, and I declare to the world what I have heard from him.

- Contradiction again. ‘I contain multitudes.’

  

**34** Jesus answered them, “Truly, truly, I say to you, every one who commits sin is a slave to sin.”

- Sin confines. Separates. Draws one away from the One. Seeks personal glory, seeks rewards for oneself, rather than complete surrender of reward, complete sacrifice. To surrender is to be free.

  

**38** I speak of what I have seen with my Father, and you do what you have heard from your father.

  

**44 …**[the devil] was a murderer from the beginning, and has nothing to do with the truth, because there is no truth in him. When he lies, he speaks according to his own nature, for he is a liar and the father of lies. … **50** Yet I do not seek my own glory; there is One who seeks it and he will be the judge.

- To glory in oneself is the ultimate lie (?), and that which begets lies, untruth.
- To be a murderer from the beginning is to separate from the beginning, to bring an end between oneself and the Whole.

  

#### Chapter 10

  

**11** I am the good shepherd. The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep. **12** He who is a hireling and not a shepherd, whose own the sheep are not, sees the wolf coming and leaves the sheep and flees; and the wolf snatches them and scatters them.

- The Pharisees as hirelings. Materialists as hirelings. The master and the emissary. The emissary cares not for the One, but for oneself, and if a threat befalls one, one will flee from the One. This is death.

  

**16** And I have other sheep, that are not of this fold; I must bring them also, and they will heed my voice. So there shall be one flock, one shepherd.

- Krishna like.

  

#### Chapter 14

**5** Thomas said to him, “Lord, we do not know where you are going; how can we know the way?” **6** Jesus said to him, “I am the way, and the truth, and the life; no one comes to the Father, but by me. **7** If you had known me, you would have known my Father also; henceforth you know him and have seen him.”

- Through the explicit we come to know the implicit. Both are folds of the same ground.

  

**10** Do you not believe that I am in the Father and the Father in me? The words that I say to you I do not speak on my own authority; but the Father who dwells in me does his works. **11** Believe me that I am in the Father and the Father in me; or else believe me for the sake of the works themselves.

- The Son as the Father’s channel, the works of which are enlivened by the Holy Spirit. Almost like Christ is Matter, the Holy Spirit is Motion, and the Father is Energy (E=mc^2)

  

**28** You heard me say to you, ‘I go away, and I will come to you.’ If you loved me, you would have rejoiced, because I go to the Father; for the Father is greater than I.

- Father is greater. Son returning to the Father, to the implicit, the implicit from which unfolded the Son.
